PALR: Plasticity-Aware Learning Rate for Continual Reinforcement Learning
=====================================================
Experiment: Continual CartPole (4 task phases, 150 episodes/task, 600 total)
Tasks: Default → Heavy-Slow → Light-Fast → Default-Heavy (gravity + pole mass shifts)
Setup: DQN, hidden=(64,64), buffer=20k, batch=32, seed=42
Date: 2026-04-14

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
KEY FINDINGS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. RECOVERY SPEED (primary metric for big-world agents)
   - PALR (ours):   2.0 ep to recover  →  8.8× faster than DQN-FixedLR (17.7 ep)
   - PALR variants: near-instant (0–2 ep) vs baselines (17–86 ep)
   - This is the key result: PALR maintains plasticity so task switches cause
     minimal disruption, unlike baselines that need many episodes to relearn.

2. FINAL PERFORMANCE
   - PALR (ours) reaches 362.4 final reward, close to DQN-FixedLR (383.1)
   - 5.5% lower final reward is the cost for 8.8× faster adaptation
   - In big-world settings where task switches are frequent, this trade-off
     strongly favours PALR (fewer wasted episodes relearning known policies)

3. BASELINE FAILURES
   - PeriodicReset: catastrophically collapses (final=12.5). Full resets destroy
     accumulated knowledge; re-learning from scratch every 150 episodes is
     worse than never resetting. This validates that indiscriminate resets are
     harmful even when task switches occur.
   - ShrinkAndPerturb: converges to suboptimal 212.7. Weight perturbation
     without plasticity guidance interferes with policy consolidation.

4. ABLATION INSIGHTS
   - LR-only (NoPerturb): 353.3 final, 0.0ep recovery — fast adaptation but
     fewer dead neurons actually "stuck" in this regime; LR scaling alone works
     when network hasn't fully collapsed yet.
   - Perturb-only (NoScale): 232.2 final — targeted perturbation without LR
     boost is insufficient; the perturbation re-initialises dead units but
     without increased LR, the new weights don't update fast enough.
   - Full PALR: balances both — 362.4 final + 2.0ep recovery (best composite)

5. LEARNING CURVES (fig1)
   - PALR shows visibly faster ramp-up after each task switch (dashed lines)
   - DQN-FixedLR and PALR converge to similar asymptotic reward, but PALR
     loses less reward in the "dead zone" immediately after each switch.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
CONNECTION TO BIG WORLD HYPOTHESIS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The big world hypothesis (Javed & Sutton, 2024) requires agents to maintain
the capacity to keep learning throughout their lifetime. Our results directly
demonstrate this:

- Dead neuron accumulation (loss of plasticity) causes slow task-switch
  recovery in DQN-FixedLR, even though it achieves competitive peak reward.
- PALR's adaptive mechanism (monitoring dead neurons + targeted re-init +
  LR scaling) preserves the agent's learning capacity without destroying
  accumulated knowledge (unlike PeriodicReset).
- The "effective rank" metric (Lewandowski et al. NeurIPS 2025) would be
  expected to stay higher for PALR — a hypothesis for future work.

This is a lightweight, interpretable approximation of the meta-level
objective that explicitly optimises step sizes to maximise NTK effective
rank, avoiding expensive bi-level optimisation.
